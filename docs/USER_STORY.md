# User story — the Halden deal

**Maya Okonkwo** is CFO of **Halden Robotics**, a warehouse-automation company that needs
growth capital. The board agrees to sell a **12% secondary stake** — 120,000 units. Two
suitors are circling: **Boranic Systems**, a larger competitor that would love a look at
Halden's books, and **Meridian Capital**, a friendly growth fund. Maya wants a real price,
but she will not hand a rival her audited financials, and she refuses to spend six weeks in
the usual data-room-plus-lawyers-plus-escrow gauntlet.

She opens the deal in **Atrium**.

### 1 · She opens the room, on her terms
Maya creates the Halden deal and uploads two documents: an **investment teaser** (tier 1) and
the **audited financials** (tier 2). She admits Boranic to **tier 1 only** — they're a
competitor, so they get the teaser and nothing more. She admits Meridian to **tier 1 and 2**.
She never has to tell either suitor who else is in the room.

### 2 · Diligence under glass
Boranic logs in and sees the teaser — and a sealed, redacted tier-2 document labelled *"not in
your tier."* They never learn Meridian exists. Meridian logs in and works through both
documents. Behind the glass, Maya watches a single feed: a **tamper-proof access trail** —
every document, every viewer, every timestamp. Boranic opened the teaser at 9:14. Meridian
opened the financials at 10:02.

Then a sensitive line from the term sheet shows up in a trade newsletter. In the old world this
is an unsolvable whodunit. Maya opens the trail: exactly one party had ever accessed that
document. Instant forensics, no investigation.

### 3 · The bids no one can peek at
Both suitors submit offers. Maya sees both — Meridian at $35/unit, Boranic at $31. Neither
suitor can see the other's bid; there is no bookrunner who could quietly tip off a favorite.
Meridian is both the higher bid and the better long-term holder.

### 4 · One click, and it's done
Maya accepts Meridian's offer. She hits **Settle**. In a **single transaction**, Meridian's
$4.2M in tokenized cash moves to Halden *and* the 12% tokenized equity moves to Meridian —
atomically. There is no escrow agent, no two-week wire-and-pray, no window where the money has
left but the shares haven't arrived. Either both legs move together or nothing moves at all.

Off to the side, the **regulator's node** confirms the close happened and that the allocation
matched the bids that were actually recorded — without ever seeing Halden's financials.

### The before/after
What used to be Datasite + DocuSign + a law firm + an escrow agent + a transfer agent, strung
together over six weeks, happened in **one afternoon, in one place** — with a provable chain of
custody running unbroken from the first document view to final settlement. Maya showed each
party exactly what they were owed and nothing more, proved who saw what, and closed without ever
being half-exposed.

That's the whole pitch in one sentence: **the permissioned folder finally grew up and learned
to close the deal.**
